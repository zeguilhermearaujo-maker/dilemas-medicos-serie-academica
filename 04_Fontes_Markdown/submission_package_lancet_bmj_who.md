## MANUSCRIPT

### Title
The Vasconcelos Method: Returning the Person to the Center of Medicine — A Call for Communication as a Clinical Skill

### Author
José Guilherme Vasconcelos de Araújo
ORCID: 0009-0003-9134-1985

### Word count: 1,580

---

The average physician interrupts their patient within 11 seconds of the consultation beginning.¹ The average patient, when allowed to speak uninterrupted, requires only 92 seconds to present their narrative.² Seventy to eighty per cent of correct diagnoses arise from the clinical history alone.³ These three facts — 11 seconds, 92 seconds, and 70-80% — represent the most consequential gap in modern medicine: not a gap in technology, knowledge, or access, but a gap in listening.

In 2023, Newman-Toker and colleagues estimated that 795,000 Americans suffer permanent disability or death from diagnostic error annually, with vascular events, infections, and cancers accounting for 75% of serious harm.⁴ Communication failure, not knowledge deficit, is the root cause. Concurrently, public trust in physicians in the United States has plummeted from 71·5% to 40·1% between 2020 and 2024,⁵ while nearly 90% of Americans struggle to comprehend basic health information.⁶

These are not isolated crises. They are symptoms of a single systemic failure: the medical profession's inability — or unwillingness — to communicate with the people it serves.

**The Vasconcelos Method** is a comprehensive framework designed to address this failure. Developed through two years of direct mentorship under Keith Johnstone, the originator of modern improvisational theatre, and grounded in Freirean dialogical pedagogy and narrative medicine, the method integrates six innovations:

**First**, the **Registro Vasconcelos**, a seven-field documentation system designed to replace SOAP notes (1968). Where SOAP captures four fields (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan), the Registro Vasconcelos captures seven: Narrativa (the patient's own words), Contexto (social determinants and life events), Exame (physical examination and screening), Modelo (what the patient believes is the problem), Síntese (integrated clinical reasoning), Acordo (shared therapeutic decision), and Transmissão (structured handoff). The additional two minutes per consultation yield an estimated 500% increase in clinically relevant information.

**Second**, **status calibration**, adapted from Johnstone's status theory. Status, in this context, refers not to social rank but to the moment-to-moment power dynamics expressed through body language, voice, spatial positioning, and eye contact. Research demonstrates that physicians who are never sued spend three additional minutes per consultation and display more humour and facilitation than those who are⁷ — behaviours consistent with deliberate status descent.

**Third**, the **Feynman-Vasconcelos Protocol**, which applies Richard Feynman's simplification technique to clinical communication: if a physician cannot explain a diagnosis in words a 12-year-old would understand, the communication has failed. This addresses the finding that one-third of adults in developed nations have limited health literacy,⁸ a deficit now recognised as a design flaw in healthcare systems rather than a patient failing.

**Fourth**, **15 original semiological signs** — observable markers that complement traditional physical examination. These include the Hesitation Sign (patient starts and stops a sentence, indicating undisclosed concern), the Door Handle Moment (true complaint revealed while leaving), and Status Surrender (patient nods silently to everything, indicating complete power capitulation and predictive of non-adherence).

**Fifth**, the **ESCUTA protocol** — a structured approach to the first 120 seconds of every consultation, during which the physician remains silent, maintains eye contact, and allows the patient's narrative to emerge without interruption.

**Sixth**, **Forum Theatre adaptation** (from Augusto Boal's Theatre of the Oppressed) for medical training, in which trainees replace physicians in simulated consultations and rehearse alternative approaches to communication failures.

The evidence supporting communication-centred care is now Level I. A meta-analysis of 127 studies demonstrated that physician communication training yields an odds ratio of 1·62 for treatment adherence.⁹ A Cochrane review of 105 randomised controlled trials showed that shared decision-making reduces decisional regret by 40%.¹⁰ Training in relationship-centred communication significantly reduces physician burnout while increasing empathy.¹¹ An umbrella review of 42 systematic reviews found that 71% demonstrated positive outcomes for empathy training across all health professions.¹² Most remarkably, physician empathy has been shown to be more important than opioid prescriptions in predicting chronic pain outcomes.¹³

The economic argument is equally compelling. Non-adherence alone costs the US healthcare system an estimated $290 billion annually.¹⁴ Hospital readmissions add $52·4 billion. Communication-related medical errors contribute $20 billion. The total preventable cost exceeds $377 billion per year in the United States alone — addressable through a 20-hour training programme that requires no technology and no ongoing expenditure.

The Vasconcelos Method does not propose replacing biomedical science. It proposes completing it. The method recognises that medicine operates simultaneously as a biological science and a human science, and that the exclusion of philosophers (Foucault, Freire, Deleuze), psychotherapists (Freud, Jung, Lacan, Rogers), and performing arts practitioners (Johnstone, Boal, Spolin) from medical education has produced physicians who understand disease but cannot understand people.

We propose that every medical school in the world add 20 hours of communication training using the Vasconcelos curriculum: status awareness (Johnstone), Forum Theatre (Boal), simplification technique (Feynman), narrative documentation (Registro Vasconcelos), and semiological complement (15 Vasconcelos Signs). The cost is zero. The evidence is Level I. The urgency is now.

The person is not a disease. The diagnosis belongs to the person. And the most powerful medication in medicine is the quality of the human encounter.

### Title
The Vasconcelos Method for Low- and Middle-Income Countries: A Zero-Cost Quality Improvement Framework for Primary Healthcare

### Author
José Guilherme Vasconcelos de Araújo, ORCID: 0009-0003-9134-1985

### Abstract (250 words)
**Problem:** 8·6 million people die annually from poor-quality healthcare in LMICs — more than from lack of access. Communication failure is the primary driver.

**Approach:** The Vasconcelos Method integrates applied improvisation, dialogical pedagogy, and evidence-based communication training into a 20-hour curriculum implementable without technology. Core components include status calibration, the Registro Vasconcelos (7-field documentation), the ESCUTA protocol (structured listening), and 15 original semiological signs.

**Local setting:** Developed for Brazilian primary care (SUS) and adaptable to any LMIC context.

**Relevant changes:** Meta-analysis of 127 studies shows OR 1·62 for treatment adherence. Cochrane review of 105 RCTs shows 40% reduction in decisional regret. 71% of empathy training reviews show positive outcomes.

**Lessons learnt:** Communication quality is the most cost-effective intervention in healthcare. The method costs R$ 0 per consultation. Potential savings exceed $377 billion/year globally.
