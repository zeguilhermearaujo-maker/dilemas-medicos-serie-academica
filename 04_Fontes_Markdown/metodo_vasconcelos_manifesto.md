## PART I: THE FEYNMAN PROTOCOL FOR MEDICINE

### 1.1. What Feynman Taught the World

Richard Feynman (1918-1988), Nobel laureate in Physics, developed a learning technique:

> **"If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough."**

Feynman's 4 steps:
1. Choose a concept
2. Explain it as if teaching a child — no jargon, only simple words
3. Identify where you get stuck — those are your knowledge gaps
4. Go back, learn, simplify again

### 1.2. The Medical Feynman Problem

In medicine, the Feynman principle is REVERSED. Physicians are trained to use increasingly COMPLEX language — not to understand better, but to SIGNAL STATUS. Medical jargon is not a tool of precision. It is a tool of POWER.

| What the physician says | What the patient hears | What should be said |
|------------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| "You have a myocardial infarction" | Nothing — panic, confusion | "Part of your heart muscle didn't get enough blood. We need to fix that NOW" |
| "The MRI shows a lesion" | "I have cancer" (fear) | "The scan shows a spot we need to look at more carefully. There are many things it could be" |
| "Your HbA1c is 9.2" | Nothing — numbers without meaning | "Your blood sugar over the last 3 months has been too high. It's like running a car engine too hot — it damages things slowly" |
| "We need to rule out PE" | Nothing — or terror | "We need to make sure there isn't a blood clot in your lungs. That's why we're doing this test" |
| "You have MDD with comorbid GAD" | Labeled, not understood | "You're going through a very difficult time. You're feeling deeply sad AND constantly worried. Let's talk about what might help" |
| "Your prognosis is guarded" | "I'm going to die?" | "We're in a difficult situation. I want to be honest with you about what we're facing, and I want to hear what matters most to you" |
| "Bilateral crepitations on auscultation" | Nothing | "When I listen to your lungs, I can hear crackling sounds on both sides. That usually means there's fluid where there shouldn't be" |
| "NPO after midnight" | Nothing | "Don't eat or drink anything after midnight — your stomach needs to be empty for the surgery to be safe" |
| "Idiopathic" | Nothing | "We don't know the cause yet. But that doesn't mean we can't treat it" |
| "The biopsy was benign" | Relief, but uncertain | "Great news — the sample we took is NOT cancer. It's harmless" |

### 1.3. The FEYNMAN-VASCONCELOS Protocol

| Step | Feynman original | Vasconcelos clinical adaptation |
|------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| 1 | Choose a concept | Before speaking to the patient, ask yourself: "What is the ONE thing I need this person to understand right now?" |
| 2 | Explain it to a child | Explain using ONLY words a 12-year-old would understand. If you must use a medical term, IMMEDIATELY translate it |
| 3 | Identify knowledge gaps | Watch the patient's face. If they look confused, YOU have failed — not them. Ask: "Does this make sense so far?" |
| 4 | Simplify again | If they didn't understand, find a BETTER analogy. Use their world — their job, their family, their daily life — as the reference frame |

### 1.4. The Feynman Test for Every Consultation

Before the patient leaves, the physician must be able to answer YES to:

1. Can the patient explain their condition in their OWN words?
2. Can they explain WHY they are taking each medication?
3. Can they explain what to do if they get worse?
4. Can they explain when to come back?

**If the answer to any of these is NO, the consultation FAILED — regardless of how technically correct the diagnosis was.**

## PART II: MEDICAL JARGON AS A STATUS WEAPON

### 2.1. The Evidence of Harm

| Fact | Datum | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| Adults with limited health literacy in developed nations | ~33% (one-third) | WHO, 2024; multiple studies |
| Health literacy is NOT a patient deficit — it is a design flaw | System-level issue, not individual failure | ResearchGate, Dec 2024 |
| Consequences of jargon | Medication errors, missed diagnoses, poor self-management, avoidable hospitalizations | WHO, 2024 |
| Patients who don't understand discharge instructions | 40-80% forget information immediately | Kessels, 2003, J Royal Soc Med |
| Language barriers increase adverse events | Patients with limited proficiency = more adverse events, reduced satisfaction | NIH systematic review |
| Words can heal or harm | Language shapes the physician-patient relationship — builds trust or perpetuates harm | NIH, pub. Dec 2025 |
| Jargon in research summaries is pervasive | Even "plain language summaries" contain excessive jargon | JMIR, Jan 2025 |

### 2.2. Why Physicians Use Jargon (The Status Analysis)

| Reason (conscious) | Real reason (unconscious, per Johnstone) | Effect on patient |
|--------------------|----------------------------------------|-------------------|
| "Precision" | Establishing expertise = high status | Patient feels ignorant, submissive |
| "Efficiency" | Avoiding the effort of translation | Patient doesn't understand, doesn't adhere |
| "Professional culture" | Habitus (Bourdieu): reproducing what was modeled | Perpetuating exclusion across generations |
| "Legal protection" | Documentation in medical language | Patient can't read their own chart |
| "Time saving" | Not wanting to explain | Patient goes home confused, returns sicker |

### 2.3. The Vasconcelos Translation Table

| Medical jargon | Human language |
|---------------|---------------|
| Myocardial infarction | Heart attack — part of your heart didn't get blood |
| Cerebrovascular accident (CVA) | Stroke — blood flow to your brain was interrupted |
| Neoplasm | Growth — could be cancer, could be harmless. We need to check |
| Idiopathic | We don't know the cause yet |
| Benign | NOT cancer. Harmless |
| Malignant | Cancer. Let's talk about what we can do |
| Prognosis | What we expect will happen |
| Etiology | The cause |
| Differential diagnosis | The list of possibilities we're considering |
| Bilateral | Both sides |
| Crepitations | Crackling sounds in the lungs |
| Edema | Swelling from fluid |
| Dyspnea | Difficulty breathing |
| Tachycardia | Heart beating too fast |
| Hypertension | High blood pressure |
| Hypoglycemia | Blood sugar too low |
| Metastasis | Cancer has spread to another part of the body |
| Palliative | Focused on comfort and quality of life |
| NPO | Don't eat or drink |
| PRN | As needed |
| BID/TID/QID | Twice/three times/four times a day |
| Contraindicated | Not safe to use |
| Iatrogenic | Caused by the treatment itself |
| Comorbidity | Other health conditions you also have |
| Polypharmacy | Taking many medications |
| HbA1c | Average blood sugar over 3 months |
| INR | How fast your blood clots (for warfarin) |
| GFR | How well your kidneys are working |
| BMI | A number based on height and weight |
| SSRI | A type of antidepressant that works on serotonin |
